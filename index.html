Primary Sources

Explore authentic documents and materials written by or about key figures like Omar ibn Said, Nicholas Said, Job (Ayyub) the son of Solomon, and more.

Omar Ibn Said. The Autobiography of Omar Ibn Said: A Bilingual Edition.

Bilingual Edition of Omar Ibn Said

This bilingual edition presents the autobiography of Omar Ibn Said, an enslaved African Muslim scholar in the United States. Written in Arabic, it is a unique narrative that blends personal experience with Islamic teachings. The work offers insights into Omar's intellectual and spiritual resilience despite the harsh realities of slavery. The bilingual format enhances accessibility and provides a deeper understanding of Omar’s original text and its cultural context.

Said, Nicholas. The Autobiography of Nicholas Said.

The autobiography of Nicholas Said

This autobiography narrates the extraordinary life of Nicholas Said, a freed African slave who became a world traveler and educator. Said chronicles his early life in West Africa, his enslavement, and eventual emancipation, providing a rare perspective on the experiences of African Muslims in the 19th century. The text explores themes of identity, perseverance, and adaptation, offering a remarkable look at cross-cultural encounters.

Bluett, Thomas. Some Memoirs of the Life of Job (Ayyub ibn Suleiman Diallo).

Some Memoirs of the Life of Job(Ayyub ibn Suleiman Diallo

Bluett recounts the story of Ayyub ibn Suleiman Diallo, a Muslim scholar from West Africa who was enslaved in the Americas and later freed. The narrative provides a detailed account of Job’s journey, from his capture to his return to Africa, highlighting his unwavering Islamic faith and intellectual prowess. This memoir is a vital historical document shedding light on the intersection of slavery and religion in the 18th century.

Diouf, Sylviane A. Servants of Allah: African Muslims Enslaved in the Americas..

Diouf, Sylviane A. Servants of Allah: African Muslims Enslaved in the Americas.

Diouf's seminal work explores the lives of African Muslims who were enslaved in the Americas, highlighting their cultural, religious, and social contributions. The book examines how these individuals maintained their Islamic practices and identity despite the oppressive conditions of slavery. The 15th-anniversary edition includes updated research, providing a comprehensive study of a marginalized group within the history of slavery.

Ball, James. The Story of Yarrow Mamout: From Slave Ship to Harvard.

Ball, James. The Story of Yarrow Mamout: From Slave Ship to Harvard.

This biography traces the life of Yarrow Mamout, a former African slave who became a prominent figure in 18th-century Georgetown. The book delves into his journey from enslavement to freedom, exploring themes of resilience, community, and cultural heritage. Through Yarrow’s story, Ball uncovers the broader history of African-American families and their contributions to American society over generations.

Secondary Sources

Dive deeper into the history of African Muslims in the Americas through articles, videos, and research papers.

Videos

  • Fighting for Freedom: The Little-Known Story of Muslims and the Civil War

    This documentary-style video highlights the involvement of Muslims(Nicholas Said) in the Civil War, showcasing their contributions and struggles during the era. It offers a rarely discussed perspective on Muslim participation in shaping American history and explores their fight for freedom and justice amidst a predominantly Christian social context.

  • From Slavery to Freedom: The Untold Story of America's First Muslims(Yarrow Mamout)

    This video traces the journey of America’s first Muslims (Yarrow Mamout), many of whom were enslaved Africans, and their cultural and religious influence on early American society. The narrative examines their resilience and the challenges of preserving their faith under slavery.

  • The opera "Omar," on a Muslim slave in America

    This video explores the opera based on Omar Ibn Said, dramatizing his life as an enslaved Muslim scholar in America. The production creatively conveys themes of identity, spirituality, and resistance, emphasizing the human experience of slavery.

Research & Articles

  • Research Paper: Private Kahn and Brother Webb: Islam in the Late 19th-Century United States.

    This scholarly article investigates the presence of Islam in late 19th-century America through the experiences of Private Kahn and Brother Webb. It explores how Islam was perceived and practiced during this time, highlighting the persistence of Muslim identity despite societal pressures.

  • Review by Amina Wadud – Servants of Allah: African Muslims Enslaved in the Americas by Sylviane A. Diouf. JSTOR

    Wadud’s review praises Diouf’s work for its meticulous research and detailed portrayal of African Muslims’ lives under slavery. The review underscores the importance of Diouf’s scholarship in filling gaps in historical narratives and its significance in understanding cultural and religious resistance

  • African American Muslims in Early America. National Museum of African American History and Culture (NMAAHC)

    This online resource provides a collection of stories about enslaved Muslims in America, emphasizing their cultural heritage and religious practices. It connects individual biographies to broader historical contexts, enriching our understanding of African Muslim contributions to early America.

  • Yarrow Mamout: The fascinating story of an early American Muslim slave.

    This article tells the story of Yarrow Mamout, a former slave who became a prominent figure in early American society. It discusses his resilience, financial independence, and legacy as an early African Muslim in the U.S.

  • The Untold Stories of Enslaved African Muslim Women in the Americas.

    This article explores the overlooked experiences of enslaved African Muslim women in the Americas. It discusses their spiritual resilience, cultural contributions, and the challenges they faced in maintaining their identities under oppressive conditions.

  • Enslaved and Freed Muslim Slave: Spiritual Wayfarers in the South and Lowcountry – Omar ibn Said (1770–1864).

    This exhibit examines the life of Omar Ibn Said and other African Muslims in the South, focusing on their spiritual journeys and cultural impact. It highlights their role as "spiritual wayfarers" in preserving Islam and enriching Southern society.

  • A Statement with Regard to the Moorish Prince, Abduhl Rahhahman / by T.H. Gallaudet.

    This document recounts the life of Abdul Rahman Ibrahima Sori, a West African prince enslaved in America. It provides valuable insights into his royal heritage, struggles under slavery, and eventual liberation, highlighting his intellectual and cultural contributions.

Contemporary Voices

  • Who Was Omar ibn Said? North Carolina’s Enslaved Muslim Scholar.

    This article provides a comprehensive overview of Omar Ibn Said's life, emphasizing his scholarship, religious identity, and the cultural tensions he navigated as a Muslim slave in America.

  • The Moor’s Account by Laila Lalami.

    Lalami’s historical novel imagines the life of Mustafa ibn Muhammad al-Zamori (Estebanico), one of the first African Muslims in the Americas. The book combines historical fiction with themes of identity, resilience, and survival, enriching the narrative of Muslim contributions to early America.
